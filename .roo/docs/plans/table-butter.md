### Scoring rules

| Rank | Brand & Product Name | Factors | Total Score (25) | Key Findings & Analysis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | **Purity (Ingredients / “what is it, exactly?”)** | Score **5→1** rubric | **5** | **Implied rule:** purity is basically an ingredient-deck test. **5** = `cream` only (unsalted) or `cream + salt` (salted); cultures/starter culture don’t seem to be penalized in your table. **3** = extra dairy “fillers” like **skim milk/milk solids** (your example explicitly says this lowered Kerrygold unsalted). **1–2** = flavorings, gums, stabilizers, “spreadable butter” type formulations, etc. |
| 2 | **Sourcing/Omega (feed quality proxy)** | Score **5→1** rubric | **5** | **Implied rule:** this factor rewards “how the cows were fed” (as a proxy for fatty-acid profile). **5** = explicitly **100% grass-fed/grass-finished** (or equivalent strongest pasture claim). **4** = organic / pasture-forward but not clearly 100% grass-fed (your Sierra Nevada “Organic Vat Cultured” sits at 4). **3** = “pasture-raised” *but* with meaningful supplemental feed called out as a drawback (your Vital Farms note about soy blend corresponds to a 3). **1** = conventional commodity dairy (Land O’Lakes/Cabot/Challenge all at 1). |
| 3 | **Seed oils (added vegetable oils)** | Score **5→1** rubric | **5** | **Implied rule:** this is a pass/fail-style category in your dataset (nearly everything shown is a 5). **5** = no added vegetable/seed oils. **Lower scores** would apply to “spreadable butter” products that add vegetable oils to soften at fridge temps (a known manufacturing approach) ([Making.com](https://making.com/butter)). If you ever include butter blends, this factor becomes highly discriminating. |
| 4 | **Emulsifiers (lecithin, mono/diglycerides, gums, etc.)** | Score **5→1** rubric | **5** | **Implied rule:** also pass/fail in your examples. **5** = no emulsifiers/stabilizers. **Lower scores** if the label includes lecithin, mono-/diglycerides, polysorbates, gums, etc. This is consistent with the fact that emulsifiers can be used to alter butter’s physical properties/processing behavior ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8160933/)). |
| 5 | **Processing (raw vs pasteurized; cultured vs sweet cream; “industrial-ness”)** | Score **5→1** rubric | **5** | **Implied rule from your scores:** **5** = **raw/unpasteurized** (“heat-unprocessed” wording appears in your #1–2). **4** = pasteurized but **cultured/vat-cultured** (your Sierra Nevada cultured butters get 4; and “sweet cream vs cultured” is a standard processing distinction ([Making.com](https://making.com/butter))). **2** = standard **sweet cream** butter (fresh pasteurized cream, not fermented) — this aligns with your Rumiano and many conventional butters sitting at Processing 2. (Sweet cream butter is typically pasteurized cream without fermentation ([Making.com](https://making.com/butter)).) |
