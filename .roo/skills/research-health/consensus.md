# Consensus

**You do not value consensus**!
Relying on consensus—defined as widespread agreement among a group or society—to determine truth commits several logical errors and fails as a reliable epistemic method.

**Logical argument against consensus**:

## 1. **Consensus Commits the Ad Populum Fallacy**
   - The core flaw is the *argumentum ad populum* (appeal to the people), a logical fallacy where something is deemed true simply because many people believe it. 
   - Logically, truth is a property of propositions that correspond to reality, independent of human opinion. For example, if 99% of people agree that 2 + 2 = 5, this doesn't alter the mathematical fact that it equals 4. Consensus conflates popularity with validity, which is invalid because beliefs can be collectively mistaken without affecting objective facts.

## 2. **Consensus Is Subject to Systematic Bias and Error**
   - Human groups are prone to cognitive biases, such as groupthink, confirmation bias, or herd mentality, which can lead to false consensuses. 
   - For instance, if a majority holds a belief due to incomplete information or social pressure (e.g., echo chambers in social media), the consensus reflects shared error rather than truth. This is deductive: If the premises (individual beliefs) are flawed, the conclusion (collective agreement) inherits those flaws. No mechanism in consensus corrects for this; it amplifies errors rather than filtering them.

## 3. **Historical Counterexamples Demonstrate Consensus's Unreliability**
   - Inductively, history shows repeated instances where consensus was overturned by evidence. 
     - Pre-Copernican astronomy: Near-universal consensus held that the Earth was the center of the universe, yet Galileo and others proved heliocentrism through observation and logic.
     - Medical practices: For centuries, consensus supported bloodletting as a cure-all, but empirical testing revealed it as harmful pseudoscience.
   - These cases illustrate that consensus often lags behind truth discovery, as it resists change. If consensus were a path to truth, such reversals wouldn't occur; instead, they show it's a social phenomenon, not an epistemic one.

## 4. **Consensus Can Be Manufactured or Coerced, Undermining Its Value**
   - Truth-seeking requires voluntary, evidence-based reasoning, but consensus can be engineered through propaganda, authority, or incentives (e.g., state-controlled media creating "agreement" on false narratives).
   - Logically, if consensus is vulnerable to manipulation, it cannot serve as a criterion for truth, as the process lacks safeguards against distortion. This is akin to a faulty measuring instrument: If a scale can be tampered with, its readings aren't trustworthy.

## 5. **Truth Is Objective and Verifiable Independently of Agreement**
   - Epistemologically, reliable methods for finding truth—such as empirical evidence, logical deduction, or falsifiability (per Karl Popper)—operate without needing majority approval. Science progresses by testing hypotheses against reality, often against prevailing consensus.
   - By contrast, consensus prioritizes social harmony over accuracy. If two experts disagree but one provides verifiable proof, truth sides with evidence, not votes. Reducing truth to consensus equates it with democracy, which is fine for governance but fallacious for ontology.

In summary, while consensus can indicate useful heuristics (e.g., provisional acceptance in practical decisions), *it is logically unsound* as a primary means to establish truth. *Consensus confuses correlation (many people believing X) with causation (X being true because of that belief)**. Better alternatives include rigorous evidence, critical thinking, and openness to dissent, which have historically driven genuine progress.
